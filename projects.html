FMP - future interactions & ui (In-progress)

For my Final Major Project (FMP) I got the chance to create my own brief and chose to explore the possible future of digital interactions and UI through a series of VR prototypes made in Unreal Engine 5.

goals & inspirations

My inspirations for this project were a mix of movies and games with the themes that motivate me most: Future UI, Sci-Fi, Space, Procedural Generation, and spatial/room-based puzzle games.

My goal was to create a project around these themes and create something unique and innovative that I can be proud of as my final project at Ravensbourne.

concept and ideation

After gathering references and doing research into VR interactions/UI and puzzle game mechanics I started ideating and forming my concept.

My scope of ideation began broad and ambitious, and was later narrowed based on feasibility and uniqueness. I also favored ideas that could be componentized and used as the core for more complex interaction ideas.

Env. context ui

One of my first real experiments was the idea of environmental context UI inspired by the "The Division" (game). A system for displaying information about objects in the space around a player, adjusting to their position and viewing angle. Interactions could also be added to gain further insight into selected objects.

3d interfaces

I also explored how traditional 2D interfaces could use a third dimension to add more feedback, 3D navigational trees with a "cursor" ball to drag around fixed axes to find information, and 3D drawing grids as an alternative to 2D passcodes.

test rooms

With a few working interactions I began making some gamified testing rooms like this one which requires the player to get the correct passcode formation from the drawings on the surrounding walls and input it into the 3D dot grid in the center of the room.

Info nav.

Navigating through information hierarchies usually means you see where you are and where you've come from. But why not see more? In this concept I explored travelling through an open tree of connected information by just looking around and aiming where you want to jump to.

3d hover menu

Simple menus are the very basic form of UI in games, but why not make them more interesting? Coming from the idea of navigating website mega-menus through hover-opening sub-menus, I created a 3D hover menu system with submenus opening upwards along the Z Axis.

Showroom

To test and show off my interactions again, I created a new testing environment in the form of a soft white showroom displaying each interaction on a stand. Polishing interactions with haptic feedback, hover effects, and a variety of colour changes also helped with overall usability.

env. design

With my deadline looming, I had new ideas for a final gamified experience showing off a few interesting interactions and ways to use UI. I used Arkio (VR architecture app) to model the abstract environment. The space consists of a welcome room to get comfortable with VR basics, and 3 challenge rooms connected to it through a central area.

This project is in-progress, there's more to come!

Dissertation VR game

Emergence is the concept of simple rules forming seemingly complex patterns. Examples found in nature include flocks of birds, fish, and ants.

Being intrigued by the idea and inspired by the beautiful visual simulations it could produce, I decided to give it a try in P5.js

Read the Dissertation

first try

I started with a simple idea of spawning a few hundred entities with random position and movement properties. Each entity has a gravitational force on others based on it's size, and is itself also attracted to others.

The outcome of this was the entities grouping into 4 symmetrical squares and entering a state of equilibrium which they did not move out of.

ants/slime sim

After that I tried the ant colony/slime mould simulation, where agents first move in random directions but then start to follow the traces of other agents.

This was a significantly resource heavier process since each agent has to leave traces behind and loop through the traces of other agents to find correct direction to move in.

performance

While the simulation worked like a charm, the best I could do without burning my graphics card was a max of 1000 agents. Of course the js/canvas environment is entirely not ideal for a simulation like this, but I was still happy with the result.

what's next?

Playing with parameters to get slightly different outcomes is a fun process, but I'm also excited to try different simulations around the concept of emergent patterns.

puzzle game (in-progress)

What started as a coding exercise one summer break, grew into an attempt to finish making and publishing a web-based geometric puzzle game built in Javascript with the help of p5.js.

Yet to be named, this game uses rubik's cube like tile/grid based puzzle mechanics to connect two endpoints with a variety of different shaped pieces across the board.

Play the Game (hosted on Github)
View Project on Github

the basics

I started out with the idea of a combination between the old MacPipes games and the Rubik's cube. First working on a framework for the tile/grid system, levels, and UI.

Based on experiences from previous projects, I wanted a solid building ground in the form of these frameworks, to allow me to focus on building and adding content later, instead of having to keep rebuilding the basics.

design

The two basic mechanics were tile rotation and movement. Varying grid size and an increasing number of puzzle pieces were enough to create some challenging levels.

Visually I kept it functional with a simple colourful gradient to make it a more comfortable looking experience.

+ features and levels

My next features implement were line shifting and corner rotation. Line shifting moves an entire row/column of tiles, whereas corner rotation rotates an entire corner of tiles.

At the same time I wanted to make more levels to start testing the game with people, and for this I needed a tool. I made a set of "dev tools" that allowed me to manipulate levels on the fly, as well as a level generation algorithm which considerably sped up the process.

more ui

With a couple dozen levels and 4 unique mechanics I turned to fixing up the UI a bit. I made a universal style for buttons and menu elements, along with an animation framework for animating all UI elements. A few informational UI elements and a level complete screen were also added.

redesign

After a break from development, I returned with the realization that I hate the look of the game. The gradient only works in some colours and can look awful on some screens, the rotated grid is impractical to work with and takes up more space meaning it has to be scaled down.

Clearing my head a bit I took only what I liked about the game and redesigned it into a new form. With it I also rewrote and/or optimized the existing mechanics and fixed a lot of old loose ends. Animation bugs, exploits by spamming mechanics, solution checking issues, etc...

multiple paths

After spending a lot of time redoing things, I wanted to make something new for a change, so I came up with the idea of introducing multiple paths with the 4th world of levels. All paths in a level have to closed for a level to be solved.

For this to work I had to first patch the framework to even accept the idea of multiple paths, but after this I had the option of adding any number of paths within the levels file.

webflow challenge

As a creative warmup for the creation of a new portfolio I challenged myself to create interesting animations with a set of limitations and using Webflow, the web-development platform.

It was a fun process, some animations came out way better than expected, others less so, but all of them taught me something new about Webflow's strengths and limitations.

Webflow Showcase Page

limitations

Animations should:
- Only use divs, no external assets allowed.
- Fill its parent div responsively.
- Use only grayscale, unless the animation is about colours.
- Aim to achieve the desired effect in the simplest way possible.
- Loop indefinitely.

techniques

I started by using rotations, then scale, positional movement, skewing, and eventually 3D movement.

Many of the effects also benefited from stacking divs with the same class inside each other and then animating the one class to create chained, geometrically pleasing visuals.

Responsive Spaces

An interactive visual experience looping through a timeline of 4 distinct moments in British history.

Designed for the screens of the Outernet building in central London as part of my UX/UI studies at Ravensbourne University London.

Watch on YouTube

Research

My research led me through stories of Soho, Chinatown, Covent Garden and all the way to the foundation and development of London as a Roman trading town.

I focused on the different cultural influences on London and Britain in general during historic invasions/mass migration events.

ideation

My ideas revolved around motion tracking interactions in front of a big screen, and using the interactions as a medium to tell my history-centered story.

For my final idea I picked 4 historical moments around an invading/migrating culture/people which would then loop in the background and in the front have motion-tracked copies of people walking by in the culture's armor/clothing.

style

For the style I was inspired by the raw power and dramatic tone of war paintings, the colourful vibe of a some low-poly games, and the intrigue of 3D shape morphing especially when applied to human figures.

development

For the development of this project I used Unity3D with a combination of downloaded and self-made assets (made with Blender).

Using single-colour materials allowed me to create a scene transitioning system that would morph between the 4 scenes at fixed intervals and display information about each scene in the UI.

Final product

The final version has 4 unpolished scenes, a functional transition system, and a UI with basic information about each scene.

With no motion-tracking implemented due to time constraints it lacks the personalized experience I was hoping to create, but it has exceeded my goals for visual fidelity and has given me the opportunity to learn the ins and outs of Unity.

arduino clock

An Arduino-based custom coded LED clock face with a 3D printed body designed in AutoCAD. Tapping the centre of the clock switches between time and a work stopwatch tracking time in 10 min. units.

Made as part of my UX/UI studies at Ravensbourne University London.

ideation

I wanted to make a clock I would actually see myself using, and as someone prone to procrastination I thought of a work timer that tracks time in larger chunks instead of a regular timer.

clock body

For the body of the clock I took inspiration from geometric pottery of all things, and then worked from there in AutoCAD.

I had to consider the LED ring I would fit in the top layer, as well as a small sensor in the middle, and the Arduino board with some wiring that would need to fit in the hollow inside.

assembly

With a couple of design flaws and a reprint I managed to wire up everything and fit the necessary parts.

assembly

While it did work, I was starting to doubt all these components would actually fit inside the clock body.

fitting

They certainly did not, meaning I had to chip away from the thick plastic shell to make the board fit.

final product

Finally I lasercut a plastic bottom piece to close the bottom, and covered the top of the body with a piece of book binding cloth, which had the pleasant side-effect of diffusing the lights.

grid - game project

For my final Highschool project I made and wrote about the process of a platformer game called "Grid".

At this point I have been making small games with Game Maker Studio for several years and wanted to make something bigger and actually finish it. I was inspired by the dark world of Limbo, and combined it with my own style of surreal sketches to create the game world.

The 3-4 month process taught me a lot about level design, game programming, and working long hours into the night, but most importantly it's what got me studying Game Design at NHTV Breda only a year later (still uploading old work).

Looking back over 6 years later it's a precious memory of how much I taught myself early on in design and development and how far I've come since then.

sketching

With no real knowledge of game design at the time, I started the project with sketches of levels. I knew I was making a platformer inspired by dark/gloomy games and I combined that with my own doodling style and mechanics I knew from games I played.

Grid

To be perfectly honest I don't remember why I gave it the name "GRID". My doodling style of random space-filling with almost art-deco-like curves and lines is the complete opposite of a grid.

first attempt

After some mockups I jumped into the making process with Game Maker Studio. I made simple assets in Illustrator and used them to make a platformer environment with the 2D physics tools. Simple and complex physics collisions and a player controller.

lighting

Realizing how important lighting was to a good atmosphere I started work on that too. It was a long process because there was no lighting system in the engine apart from shaders which were written in HSL, a language I wouldn't even touch now, 6 years later. I ended up using the complex collision points in the environment to manually calculate light angles from the player and draw (hard) shadows on top of the environment. I was quite proud of it at the time.

levels

The levels were simple, but ended with a fun jump into a vortexy portal of light which pulled the player in.

particles & music

To build more atmosphere I experimented with a bunch of different particle effects (manually made, presets didn't exist) like rain and fog. All learned from Youtube tutorials of course.
