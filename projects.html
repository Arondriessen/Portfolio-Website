webflow challenge

As a creative warmup for the creation of a new portfolio I challenged myself to create interesting animations with a set of limitations and using Webflow, the web-development platform.

It was a fun process, some animations came out way better than expected, others less so, but all of them taught me something new about Webflow's strengths and limitations.

Webflow Showcase Page

limitations

Animations should:
- Only use divs, no external assets allowed.
- Fill its parent div responsively.
- Use only grayscale, unless the animation is about colours.
- Aim to achieve the desired effect in the simplest way possible.
- Loop indefinitely.

techniques

I started by using rotations, then scale, positional movement, skewing, and eventually 3D movement.

Many of the effects also benefited from stacking divs with the same class inside each other and then animating the one class to create chained, geometrically pleasing visuals.

Responsive Spaces

An interactive visual experience looping through a timeline of 4 distinct moments in British history.

Designed for the screens of the Outernet building in central London as part of my UX/UI studies at Ravensbourne University London.

Watch on YouTube

Research

My research led me through stories of Soho, Chinatown, Covent Garden and all the way to the foundation and development of London as a Roman trading town.

I focused on the different cultural influences on London and Britain in general during historic invasions/mass migration events.

ideation

My ideas revolved around motion tracking interactions in front of a big screen, and using the interactions as a medium to tell my history-centered story.

For my final idea I picked 4 historical moments around an invading/migrating culture/people which would then loop in the background and in the front have motion-tracked copies of people walking by in the culture's armor/clothing.

style

For the style I was inspired by the raw power and dramatic tone of war paintings, the colourful vibe of a some low-poly games, and the intrigue of 3D shape morphing especially when applied to human figures.

development

For the development of this project I used Unity3D with a combination of downloaded and self-made assets (made with Blender).

Using single-colour materials allowed me to create a scene transitioning system that would morph between the 4 scenes at fixed intervals and display information about each scene in the UI.

Final product

The final version has 4 unpolished scenes, a functional transition system, and a UI with basic information about each scene.

With no motion-tracking implemented due to time constraints it lacks the personalized experience I was hoping to create, but it has exceeded my goals for visual fidelity and has given me the opportunity to learn the ins and outs of Unity.

AR "the division" watchface

An Augmented Reality watch-face inspired the holographic watch from the game "The Division".

Built with AR.JS and a-frame as part of my UX/UI studies at Ravensbourne University London.

learning ar

I started by learning Spark AR, Facebook's own AR software which has  streamlined the process of making face filters for Instagram.

While I managed to get The Division's signature orange outline effect for scanning effects I didn't think it was enough to build a fun experience targeted at fans of the game, which was my goal.

the new idea

I then moved to the idea of the holographic watch that agents in the game use to access the game's services.

Learning ar.js

I used codepen.io to quickly prototype and test code from the AR.JS and a-frame documentation on my phone's browser.


Using the default hero marker printed on a paper wristband I was able to get it working and from there on it was a process of learning to draw new 3D shapes and animations.

result

The final product shows 7 menu points styled fairly accurately based on the reference image from the game, contains placeholder content, but has no interactions as I originally intended.

mental health app

An app design project centered around the "5 ways to wellbeing", and how we can use "nudging" to help improve people's lives.

My app concept uses both collected and user-provided data to recommend behavioral changes and provides a way to track personal goals. Made as part of my UX/UI studies at Ravensbourne University London.

user research

Primary and secondary research was conducted to gain insights on the target-demographic's (gig-workers) work-life balance and mental health.

Affinity diagrams and user-personas were then used to come up with possible solutions, which were then evaluated with decision matrices.

user-flow

After the final idea was selected I started filtering out features, detailing processes, and connecting them to start figuring out the user-flow of the app.

prototypes & testing

With the help of user-flow diagrams, layouts were drawn and tested first through physical paper prototypes and later digitized with the Marvel app.

branding

Not wanting to disregard the data concerns around an app that collects so much data, I designed my app's brand "Transparent' around transparent data usage and giving users both access to and control over their own data.

The visual language uses dark tones with sharp accent colours and modern typography.

iterative design

Later rounds of user-tesing were also done on the digital prototypes designed with Adobe XD. Feedback was used to improve the designs and address usability concerns through iterative design.

arduino clock

An Arduino-based custom coded LED clock face with a 3D printed body designed in AutoCAD. Tapping the centre of the clock switches between time and a work stopwatch tracking time in 10 min. units.

Made as part of my UX/UI studies at Ravensbourne University London.

ideation

I wanted to make a clock I would actually see myself using, and as someone prone to procrastination I thought of a work timer that tracks time in larger chunks instead of a regular timer.

clock body

For the body of the clock I took inspiration from geometric pottery of all things, and then worked from there in AutoCAD.

I had to consider the LED ring I would fit in the top layer, as well as a small sensor in the middle, and the Arduino board with some wiring that would need to fit in the hollow inside.

assembly

With a couple of design flaws and a reprint I managed to wire up everything and fit the necessary parts.

assembly

While it did work, I was starting to doubt all these components would actually fit inside the clock body.

fitting

They certainly did not, meaning I had to chip away from the thick plastic shell to make the board fit.

final product

Finally I lasercut a plastic bottom piece to close the bottom, and covered the top of the body with a piece of book binding cloth, which had the pleasant side-effect of diffusing the lights.

digital clock

A creative coding project with the goal of learning to use Processing with Python , and developing a screen-based digital clock.

The clock uses procedural generated geometric animations in the background that changes based on the time of day and the clock mode currently in use (time, timer).

clock research

To get some inspiration for physical clock mechanisms I visited the clock installation at the Science Museum in London.

visual research

To get inspiration on abstract/geometric art I used my own photos from the Kinetic Art installation at Kunsthal Rotterdam.

ideation

Using my photos from the clock installation and ideas from other clock mechanics I tried to think of ways I could digitize them.

transitions

Taking a break from designing the clock face, I started working on a function that would animate different pre-defined shapes into each other.

I wanted to use something like this as decorative element in my clock but I wasn't yet sure how.

final clock

The final version uses procedurally generated vertices based on the time and animates shapes between them with a speed defined by the clock mode and time of day.

The clock has a time and timer mode, and has a settings menu with options to customize the animation style and theme colour.

printing passions

A project about exploring our passions, and using creative processes to produce two zine spreads about one of them.

My spread explores the game "Rainbow Six Siege", breaking down what the game is and what its appeal is to players like myself.

game research

I started by recording my gameplay and screenshotting moments I thought captured important parts of the game.

information breakdown

I also relied on mind-maps to break down details of the game, which I could then refer back to when exploring what to use for the zine.

visual experiments

For the visual part I created patterns with papers/carboards/plastics and liquids and then scanning them to create digital images I could use.

spread 1

The first spread uses a mix of patterns and roughened edges to portray the action part of the game, with the sentence every player hears when they are the last person alive from their team.

spread 2

The second spread focuses more on the strategy and information heavy part of the game, using custom iconography and informational texts about the game on the mindmap image as background.


The right half also contains a short "poem" about the game which aims to capture the complexity and intense nature of the game.
